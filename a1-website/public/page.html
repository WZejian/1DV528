    • Joy of fish

    Zhuangzi and Huizi strolled along the serene banks of a meandering river, enveloped by the tranquility of nature. The sun cast its golden rays, painting the surroundings with a serene glow. "Ah, Huizi," Zhuangzi remarked, gazing at the flowing river, "behold the boundless beauty of nature! See how the river dances over the stones, embracing its destiny without resistance. It embodies the essence of effortless action, doesn't it?" Huizi, always the skeptic, furrowed his brows. "Zhuangzi, you speak of the river's grace, but have you pondered its underlying turmoil? The water battles against the rocks, racing ceaselessly to its end. Is that not a reflection of struggle and strife?" Zhuangzi chuckled softly. "Dear Huizi, your eyes discern only conflict, missing the river's true serenity. Watch the fish," he pointed, "they glide effortlessly amidst the currents, finding joy in their aquatic realm. That, my friend, is the art of embracing life's flow." Huizi's skepticism persisted. "But Zhuangzi, how can one understand the fish's joy without being a fish? Can you truly fathom their emotions?" "Ah," Zhuangzi replied, "in seeking understanding, we must transcend our limitations. I am not a fish, indeed. Yet, just as you're not me, how can you claim to comprehend what I perceive or understand?"

    Zhuangzi, an influential Chinese philosopher and writer during the Warring States period, believed to have lived around the 4th century BCE. He is renowned for his philosophical contributions to Daoism (Taoism) and is the attributed author of the foundational Daoist text, the "Zhuangzi."
